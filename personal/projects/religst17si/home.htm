Summary

Meditation Across Traditions Offers both an academic and experiential approach to studying various Buddhist meditation traditions, including Burmese Vipassana, Chinese Pure Land, Tibetan Vajrayana and Japanese Zen. Examines the cultural and historical context of these traditions in addition to the study of their primary texts. Nine class meetings include five discussions on scholarly approaches to the study of meditation and four experiential meditation labs led by prominent meditation teachers of diverse traditions in the Bay Area.

Course Format

The nine classes for the quarter will be divided into 4 units consisting of 2 classes each, plus 1 overview class at the beginning of the quarter.

The first unit will focus on Theravada vipassana meditation, the second on Chinese Pure Land meditation, the third on Japanese Zen meditation and the fourth on Tibetan Vajrayana meditation. The first class of each unit will feature an eminent teacher representing the tradition in focus, who will offer background into the tradition, allow students to experience the techniques in the classroom, and lead a discussion based on the modern texts relevant to the tradition. The second class of each unit will complement and contrast with the first by focusing on a discussion of the primary source texts and secondary texts which describe the historical and cultural context of the tradition in question.

The cornerstone of this course lies in exploring the dissonance and critical differences between the historical and cultural contexts of the meditation traditions and their contemporary expression. By engaging both with the primary texts and scholarly literature about these traditions and with their contemporary exponents, students will begin to develop a critical capacity to evaluate the validity of historical and universal claims made about meditation and foster a wider appreciation for the broad array of practices, beliefs and worldviews that fall under the umbrella of "Buddhist meditation."

Course Requirement

•  Class participation through discussion and questions

•  Completion of all assigned readings

•  Four response papers of 2-4 pages each, due at the conclusion of each unit. Response papers focus on the differences between the historical and cultural perspectives on the meditation traditions and their modern exponents.

Required Reading List

The primary source texts will be drawn from online sources (including the Samanaphala Sutta, Anapanasati Sutta, Amitabhadhyana Sutra, Saddharmapundarika Sutra, Shobogenzo, and Kalacakra tantra sadhana) and from John S. Strong’s translations in his book, The Experience of Buddhism. Strong’s book, which includes much historical and cultural background, will also be the source of the secondary readings, which will be supplemented by a few academic journal articles (to be available in PDF on Coursework).

•  Strong, John S. 2008. The Experience of Buddhism: Sources and Interpretations. Belmont, California: Wadsworth.

The following four books will be used as background material to prepare for the “meditation lab” component of each unit. They will be supplemented by additional online and PDF readings.

•  Fronsdal, Gil. 2003. The Issue at Hand. Redwood City, California: Insight Meditation Center.

•  Hsuan, Hua. 1980. Buddha Root Farm. Burlingame, California: Buddhist Text Translation Society.

•  Suzuki, Shunryu. 1970. Zen Mind, Beginner’s Mind. New York: Weatherhill.

•  Sogyal, Rinpoche. 1998. The Tibetan Book of Living and Dying. Boston: Wisdom.
